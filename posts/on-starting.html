On starting (and the tyranny of the blank page)

May 24, 2025 · 4 min read

Every blog begins with the same impossible question: who is this for? Write for yourself and you risk navel-gazing into the void. Write for an audience and you risk performing a version of yourself that never quite fits. I've had this tab open for three weeks trying to answer it.

I think the question is wrong — or at least, it's the wrong question to start with. The better question is: what do I want to think through? Writing is thinking. The act of forcing a half-formed idea into sentences is the act of finding out whether the idea was ever real in the first place. Most of the time, it turns out it wasn't — and that's fine, that's the whole point.

So here's what I want to think through: research I'm doing, books I'm reading, music I'm learning, things I notice about the world. Plato and chess openings and why Barça's high defensive line gives me anxiety. The usual.

I've been sitting with The Republic recently and Plato keeps making the same move: he has Socrates expose the incoherence of some confident claim — about justice, knowledge, beauty — and then push toward something more precise. The process is uncomfortable for the person being questioned. It's also the most productive thing they do all day.

"The beginning is the most important part of the work." — Plato, The Republic

He's right about that, at least. The beginning matters because it sets the frame. Not because you have to get it right — you won't — but because the frame determines what questions you're allowed to ask.

My frame for this blog is something like: write honestly about what you're thinking, don't perform certainty you don't have, and don't be boring. Whether I succeed is between me and the blank page.

I have no plan to be consistent. I have no goal of building an audience. I just want a place to put things down and occasionally share them. If you're reading this: welcome. I hope something here is useful, or at least interesting. If not — I understand, I too have better things to do.
